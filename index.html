What is a tornado and how do they form?

Tornadoes are powerful and destructive rotating columns of air that extend from a thunderstorm cloud to the ground. They are often described as violent, twisting and funnel-shaped, and are capable of causing significant damage to everything in their path. Tornadoes are usually accompanied by strong winds, heavy rain, and lightning, and can occur in almost any part of the world, but they are most common in the central and southern parts of the United States, where warm, moist air from the Gulf of Mexico meets cooler air from the north. Tornadoes are measured on the Fujita scale, which ranges from F0 (weak) to F5 (extreme), based on the damage they cause. F5 tornadoes are capable of causing catastrophic damage and can have wind speeds of over 200 mph. Despite their destructive power, tornadoes typically only last a few minutes and usually have a path length of a few miles, although some can last for over an hour and travel hundreds of miles.


F1 tornado

An F1 tornado is a type of tornado that is categorized as "weak" on the Fujita scale. It has wind speeds between 73 and 112 mph, which is strong enough to cause damage to poorly constructed buildings, uproot trees, and overturn mobile homes.
Some examples of the damage that an F1 tornado can cause include:
- Shallow-rooted trees uprooted or snapped
- Damage to the roofs of poorly constructed homes and buildings
- Mobile homes overturned
- Small outbuildings, sheds, and barns damaged or destroyed
F1 tornadoes are relatively common and typically cause less damage than stronger tornadoes. However, it is still important to take tornado warnings seriously and take appropriate shelter when necessary, as even weaker tornadoes can cause significant harm and injury.

F2 tornado

An F2 tornado is a type of tornado that is categorized as "significant" on the Fujita scale. It has wind speeds between 113 and 157 mph, which is strong enough to cause considerable damage to well-constructed homes, uproot large trees, and overturn vehicles.
Some examples of the damage that an F2 tornado can cause include:
- Roofs of well-constructed homes and buildings severely damaged or torn off
- Large trees uprooted or snapped
- Cars overturned and moved considerable distances
- Mobile homes completely destroyed
F2 tornadoes are less common than weaker tornadoes, but they are still capable of causing significant damage and injuries. It is essential to take tornado warnings seriously and take appropriate shelter when necessary, regardless of the strength of the tornado..

F3 tornado

An F3 tornado is a type of tornado that is categorized as "severe" on the Fujita scale. It is considered a strong tornado, with wind speeds of between 158 and 206 mph. An F3 tornado can cause significant damage to well-built homes, overturn vehicles, and uproot trees.
Some examples of the damage that an F3 tornado can cause include:
- Homes with significant damage to roofs and exterior walls
- Large trees uprooted or snapped at the base
- Vehicles thrown and displaced
- Power poles and lines knocked down
- Significant damage to commercial buildings and warehouses F3 tornadoes are less common than weaker tornadoes, but they are still capable of causing significant destruction. It is important to take tornado warnings seriously and to take appropriate shelter when necessary, regardless of the strength of the tornado.

F4 tornado

An F4 tornado is a type of tornado that is categorized as "devastating" on the Fujita scale. It is considered a strong to violent tornado, with wind speeds of between 207 and 260 mph. An F4 tornado can cause catastrophic damage, destroying well-built homes, lifting cars off the ground and throwing them, and stripping the bark off trees.
Some examples of the damage that an F4 tornado can cause include:
- Homes and other buildings completely leveled or swept away
- Cars and other vehicles thrown and tossed like toys
- Large trees snapped and uprooted
- Heavy machinery such as trains or planes flipped over or thrown a considerable distance
- Concrete structures severely damaged or destroyed
F4 tornadoes are less common than weaker tornadoes and typically occur in smaller areas, but they are among the most powerful natural disasters on the planet. It is crucial to heed tornado warnings and take shelter in a safe place immediately when a warning is issued, regardless of the tornado's strength.

F5 tornado

An F5 tornado is the most powerful and devastating type of tornado on the Fujita scale. It is categorized as "incredible," with wind speeds of over 261 mph. An F5 tornado can cause complete destruction of well-built homes and buildings, uprooting of trees and even causing the deformation of steel-reinforced concrete structures.
Some examples of the damage that an F5 tornado can cause include:
- Houses and other structures swept away, with only the foundation left
- Cars and trucks tossed around like toys and carried great distances
- Trees debarked and stripped of branches
- Strong frame houses lifted off their foundations and leveled
- Massive objects such as boats, aircraft, and even trains tossed around
F5 tornadoes are rare but incredibly powerful and deadly, capable of causing catastrophic damage and loss of life. It is crucial to take all warnings seriously and seek shelter immediately when a tornado warning is issued, especially if it is an F5 tornado.

Safety tips


Here are some safety tips that can help you stay safe during a tornado:
1. Stay informed: Monitor local weather conditions and watch for tornado warnings. Listen to local radio or TV stations for updates and follow the advice of local authorities.
2. Have a plan: Develop a tornado safety plan with your family or coworkers. Identify safe locations in your home or workplace where you can take shelter during a tornado.
3. Know where to go: In a home, seek shelter in a basement, storm cellar, or an interior room on the lowest level of the house, away from windows. In a workplace or public building, follow the directions of emergency personnel and go to the designated shelter areas.
4. Stay away from windows: Tornado winds can cause broken glass and debris to fly through windows, which can cause serious injuries.
5. Protect your head: If you can't get to shelter, protect your head with your arms or with a helmet, a pillow, or a blanket.
6. Stay put: Once you're in a safe location, stay there until the tornado has passed and the all-clear signal has been given.
7. Stay informed: After the tornado has passed, listen to local radio or TV stations for updates on the situation and follow the advice of local authorities.
Remember that tornadoes can strike quickly and without warning, so it is essential to be prepared and take appropriate action as soon as you hear a tornado warning or see signs of a tornado. Stay alert, stay safe, and always take tornado warnings seriously.
